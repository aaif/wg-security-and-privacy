## Topics Discussed

- **Chairs meeting recap** — Alex reported two takeaways from the first cross-workgroup chairs meeting: all chairs identified a need for cross-WG collaboration, and the charter deadline of April 14th should target 3–6 month deliverables with the ability to revise later. Slack is now synced with Discord for async communication; the group may choose a primary channel in the future.
- **Mission statement adoption** — Alex proposed adopting option G: *"The Security and Privacy Working Group advances the Agentic AI Foundation's mission by establishing security and privacy as a shared discipline across the Agentic AI ecosystem. Producing open threat models, a shared taxonomy, and design patterns that providers, consumers, and peer working groups can adopt to share responsibility for trustworthy agentic AI."* Tom Sheffler voiced support. Jay White noted comfort with the wording as long as the scope clearly defines what "privacy" means in this context, suggesting "data protection" or "privacy engineering" to avoid legal implications. The group voted to adopt option G with no objections.
- **Definition of "agentic"** — Matthew Khouzam raised that "agentic" has not been formally defined by the foundation and risks drifting across working groups. Alex agreed and asked Matthew to add a proposed definition to the taxonomy section of the working document.
- **Scope review — security architecture and design patterns** — Hsiao Ying Lin proposed adding "skills" to scope item B (Security Architecture and Design Patterns). Alex accepted the addition.
- **Scope review — agent identity and trust** — The group discussed whether agent identity and trust should remain in scope or be deferred entirely to the Identity and Trust WG. Jay White argued for a complementary split: this group focuses on logging, bounding, and auditing while the Identity and Trust WG handles identity and authorization mechanisms. Alex agreed and stressed the need for clear communication between groups to avoid conflicting messaging.
- **Scope review — commerce and payment security** — Lindsay Walker, a member of both the SP and Commerce WGs, volunteered to own coordination between the two groups on commerce and payment security guardrails.
- **Scope review — data protection and privacy** — Jay White advocated for framing this as "privacy engineering" focused on technical controls (data minimization, isolation, secure memory, sensitive data handling) rather than interpreting specific regulatory requirements like HIPAA or GDPR. Ty countered that the group needs to understand regulatory requirements at a technical level to ensure systems can comply, even if the group does not interpret the law. Junjie suggested deferring regulatory alignment to the Governance and Regulatory Alignment WG. Kamil (ESET) agreed with both perspectives and added that companies also need to set their own baselines beyond regulatory floors. The group agreed to continue this discussion asynchronously in Discord.
- **Scope review — auditability and forensics** — Jari Arkko asked how the auditability scope would translate into concrete deliverables, noting the current language is high-level. Alex explained the funnel from mission to scope to deliverables and said auditability could surface as design patterns (e.g., guidance on audit logging for agents and MCPs). Specific deliverable decisions have not yet been made.
- **Threat modeling output** — Kamil questioned what the output of scope item A (threat modeling and attack surface analysis) would look like and cautioned against being too prescriptive with best practices, noting that adversaries will adapt. Alex agreed the guidance should focus on how to think about threat modeling rather than dictating exact controls, and suggested a best-practices approach covering prompt injection resistance, social engineering, and attack surface mapping as examples.
- **Compliance and regulatory considerations** — Jay suggested monitoring existing standards efforts (NIST, ISO, ENISA) and assessing their implications for agentic AI rather than duplicating regulatory work. Alex proposed partnering with other WGs to influence their deliverables with a security and privacy lens, reducing the group's commitment while maintaining influence. The group leaned toward this partnership model, with the option to expand scope later if collaboration proves insufficient.
